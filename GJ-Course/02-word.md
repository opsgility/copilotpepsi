# Exercise 2: Word — Draft, Summarize & Refine Documents (45 min)
---

Copilot in Word helps you create, summarize, and refine documents using natural language prompts. Whether you're drafting a proposal from scratch, condensing a 20-page report into an executive summary, or rewriting content for a different audience, Copilot works directly in your document.

**Key concepts:**
- **Draft mode** — In a blank document, a Copilot prompt field appears above the document. Each request creates a new numbered draft. Navigate between drafts with arrows. Select **"Keep it"** when satisfied.
- **Copilot pane** — After you start typing in a document (exiting draft mode), use the Copilot icon in the menu bar to open the Copilot pane for questions and edits.
- **File references** — Attach OneDrive files to your prompt so Copilot can use their content.

---

### Task 1: Draft a document from scratch (15 min)

You need to create a communication for employees about an upcoming change to the company's benefits enrollment process.

1. In **Word for the web**, create a blank document.
2. In the **Draft with Copilot** prompt field that appears above the document, attach the **GJ_Annual_Benefits_Summary.docx** file from your OneDrive and enter:

    **Using the attached benefits summary as reference, draft an employee communication announcing the open enrollment period for 2026. Include: key dates (enrollment opens April 1, closes April 30), what's new this year, a summary of plan options, step-by-step enrollment instructions, and who to contact with questions. Tone: friendly and clear, suitable for all employees from warehouse staff to corporate leadership. Keep it to one page.**

3. Review draft 1. The communication should be clear and complete. If it's too long or too formal, refine it:

    **Shorten this to under 500 words. Use simpler language — many of our employees work in warehouses and distribution centers. Replace any HR jargon with plain English.**

4. Review draft 2. Try one more refinement:

    **Add a "Quick Reference" table at the top with three columns: Plan Name, Monthly Cost, Key Features. Then keep the rest of the communication below it.**

5. Select **Keep it** on your preferred draft. Save the document as **GJ_Benefits_Enrollment_Communication.docx**.

### Task 2: Summarize a lengthy document (15 min)

Your manager sent you a 15-page route optimization proposal and wants a one-page summary by end of day.

1. Create a new blank document in **Word for the web**.
2. In the **Draft with Copilot** prompt, attach the **GJ_Route_Optimization_Proposal.docx** file and enter:

    **Summarize the attached route optimization proposal into a one-page executive summary. Include: the problem statement, proposed solution, expected benefits (with any specific metrics or cost savings mentioned), implementation timeline, and key risks. Format with clear headings. Tone: professional, suitable for senior leadership.**

3. Review the summary. If key details are missing, ask Copilot to enhance specific sections:

    **The "Expected Benefits" section needs more specifics. Pull the exact cost savings figures and efficiency metrics from the attached proposal and include them as bullet points.**

4. Select **Keep it** and save as **GJ_Route_Optimization_Executive_Summary.docx**.

5. Now try using the Copilot pane instead of draft mode. Click into the document and make a small edit (add your name at the top). Notice the draft mode prompt disappears. Select the **Copilot** icon in the menu bar to open the Copilot pane.

6. In the Copilot pane, ask:

    **What are the three biggest risks mentioned in this document, and what mitigations are proposed for each?**

7. Review how Copilot answers from the pane vs. draft mode — the pane provides responses you choose whether to insert, while draft mode writes directly into the document.

### Task 3: Rewrite content for a different audience (15 min)

The IT team wrote a technical infrastructure upgrade plan. You need to turn it into a version that non-technical stakeholders can understand.

1. Open the **GJ_IT_Infrastructure_Upgrade_Plan.docx** file from your OneDrive in Word for the web.

2. Select the **Copilot** icon in the menu bar to open the Copilot pane. Enter:

    **Summarize this IT infrastructure upgrade plan for a non-technical audience. Replace technical jargon with business-friendly language. Focus on: what's changing, why it matters to daily operations, what employees will notice, and the timeline. Keep it under 400 words.**

3. Review the output in the Copilot pane. If you want to insert it, select the **Copy** icon and paste it into a new document.

4. Now try rewriting for a specific audience. In the Copilot pane, enter:

    **Rewrite the "Impact on Operations" section of this document as a brief FAQ with 5 questions that warehouse managers would likely ask about this IT upgrade. Include clear, reassuring answers.**

5. Copy the FAQ output and paste it into your new document.

### Task 4: Compare documents and translate content (bonus, 10 min)

**Part A: Compare two documents**

Copilot can help you compare two documents and identify differences, conflicts, or complementary information.

1. In **Copilot Chat** (Work mode), attach both the **GJ_Route_Optimization_Proposal.docx** and the **GJ_IT_Infrastructure_Upgrade_Plan.docx** files.

2. Enter:

    **Compare these two documents. Are there any overlapping initiatives, conflicting timelines, or shared dependencies? Summarize the key differences and any areas where the two projects would need to coordinate.**

3. Review the comparison. Ask a follow-up:

    **Create a table showing the timeline, budget, and key stakeholders for each project side by side.**

**Part B: Translate content for different audiences**

1. Open the **GJ_New_Hire_Onboarding_Guide.docx** in **Word for the web**.

2. Open the Copilot pane and enter:

    **Translate the key points of this onboarding guide into Spanish. Keep the formatting with headers and bullet points. Focus on the first-week checklist and key contacts sections.**

3. Copy the translated output into a new document if needed.

4. Try another language relevant to your workforce:

    **Translate the safety and compliance section into [language]. Use simple, clear language suitable for warehouse staff.**

> [!TIP]
> Copilot can translate content into most major languages. This is especially useful for organizations with multilingual workforces — create translated versions of policies, guides, and announcements without hiring a translator.
